When Design Feels Like a Place: Lessons from Cora, the AI Email Assistant

Umaru Creative • October 10, 2025

When Design Feels Like a Place: Lessons from Cora, the AI Email Assistant

A calm inbox that feels like fresh air

Imagine opening your email and feeling relieved.
That’s the idea behind Cora, an AI email assistant that turns Gmail into something closer to an art gallery than an office tool.

With soft, Midjourney-style skies as backgrounds and painterly textures behind each message, Cora helps you manage emails — sorting, drafting replies, and archiving automatically — while keeping the space visually calm. It’s like breathing fresh air after a long meeting.

Building places, not products

The team behind Cora wrote a piece called “Build Places, Not Products”, and it captures something often forgotten in digital design.

Most SaaS tools start from features, and design comes later. Cora flips that: design leads the process.
It doesn’t aim to just look nice — it’s designed to feel different.

When sameness becomes cheap, difference becomes valuable.
Cora’s design borrows not just from app trends, but from film, architecture, fashion, and games. The result: a workspace that doesn’t just function — it transforms how you feel while working.

The product as a space

Scrolling in Cora doesn’t feel like moving through a list. It feels like walking through a textured hall, where light, motion and rhythm are deliberate.
The small friction added to scrolling makes you slow down — focusing on one thing at a time.

If you imagine the interface as a place rather than a screen, everything changes. It’s not just buttons and panels — it’s air, distance, and atmosphere.
The team describes this as designing spatial emotion — building tools that feel like environments, not spreadsheets.

When atmosphere becomes the moat

In a world where AI can generate perfect layouts in seconds, the human edge may come from something subtler — atmosphere.

  • Tools get the job done.
  • But places invite you to stay.

That’s what Cora achieves: it turns the daily act of reading emails into something calmer and more mindful. It reminds us that beyond efficiency, people crave beauty, rhythm, and a sense of space — even inside a browser tab.

Takeaway

Cora shows that design isn’t just about usability; it’s about creating an emotional climate where people feel more human.
As AI tools become more uniform, the next differentiation may not come from features, but from how a product makes people feel while using it.

When building the web, perhaps we should all ask a simple question first:
What should it feel like to be here?
